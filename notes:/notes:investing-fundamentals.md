#investing-fundamentals notes

Basic Definitions:
    Stocks: A stock is a security that represents ownership of a fraction of the corporation that issued it. The units of stock are called shares. 

    Bonds: A fixed-income investment issue by the government or corporations to raise funding. These are fixed-income securities that pays a fixed interest rate or coupon to its holders, and bond prices are inverseley correlated with interest rates. When rates go up, bond prices fall. Bonds are sold with maturity dates, by which the principal amount must be paid back in full or risk default. 

    ETFs: Exchange-traded funds are a fund that holds multiple securities or assets that are traded on the market. Unlike mutual funds, ETFs can be bought and sold on an exchange just like individual stocks.

    Indices: An index is a collection of a group of assets such as stocks and bonds that are used to track the performance of a sector of a market or a market itself. These usually consist of a collection of securities intended to replicate the performance of of a certain area of the market. 
    
    Exchanges: an exchange is a type of market for trading securities, commodities, or financial instruments. They are important in that they facilitate fair trade and hey disseminate price information. 
    
    bid/ask spread: This is the difference in price between teh highest price a buyer is willing to pay versus the lowest price a seller is willing to accept for a security. This is influenced by a variety of market factors such as market liquidity, volatility, etc.
    
    liquidity: : liquidity is a term that refers to the ease of which an asset can be converted into usable cash without a significant impact of the market value of the asset. An asset is liquif if it can be efficiently converted into ready cash.
    There are two main types of liquidity-market and accounting liquidity:
    Market Liquidity: Refers to how the extent at which a market allows assets to be bought or sold at stable, transparent prices.
    Accounting Liquidity: The easy at which an individual or a company can meet their financial obligations with the liquid assets available to them.
    
    long vs. short: A long position refers to if an investor buys and owns securities before selling them, aka the investor sells the securities outright. A short position refers to an investor who sold shares of a security, often a stock, but doesn't own them yet. The idea is that a short investor borrows shares anticipating for the stock to fall. If it does, the investor will rebuy the shares at the lower price, repar teh dealer who loaned the assets, and profit from the difference. 
    
    order types:
        Market order: Tells the broker to execute a buy/sell trade at the best available price
        Limit Order: Gives you price control by specifying the maximum you are willing to pay to buy the asset, or the minimum you are willing to accept for selling the asset. 
        Stop-loss order: Becomes a market order once a security reaches a price you specify.
        Stop-limit order: Combines the features of limit and stop orders by setting two prices. You set a stop price that activates the order and a limit price that sets you minimum acceptable price. 
        Trailing Stop order: Instead of a ficed stop price, the stop price moves as a percentage of the security value.
